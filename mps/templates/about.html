Overview of the MPS Systems Database

    • The Microphysiology Systems Database (MPS-Db) is an open source, icon driven website that aggregates preclinical and clinical data with experimentally derived MPS assay data, and provides tools to analyze and interpret MPS model results in the context of human physiology (Gough et al. 2016).

      For a quick look at what the database offers, click through the list of Key Features.

        • An Icon Driven Interface Provides Intuitive Workflow


          An icon driven interface assists in designing a study, capturing data and metadata, and analyzing assay data. Icons are arranged on the home page to provide an intuitive workflow (A. Review models, devices, compounds, and cell samples; B. Add study data; C. Perform data analysis; D. Perform computational modeling).

        • Find Information Quickly Using the Global Search Function


          The global search function simplifies the process of finding information. Enter a compound, a disease, a device, or other key words (e.g., Cmax) in the global search box and click Search to quickly locate matching content in the database.

        • Learn About MPS Models Being Used in Translational Medicine


          Visit the MPS Models page and scroll through the list of models in use by researchers working at the leading edge of the field. Select View to access detailed information on a model's design and construction. Scroll down to the Organs and Centers table below to see a list of institutions with models in the MPS-Db.

        • Explore Compound Properties


          Review compound information, including LogP, molecular weight, compound synonyms, and a link to the compound in ChEMBL, all presented in a searchable and sortable table. Select View to access additional compound properties.

        • Compare Adverse Drug Related Events


          Using data from the FDA Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) the CDC's National Ambulatory Medical Care Survey (NAMCS) database, adverse events associated to one or more compounds can be compared. Sort and search tools facilitate rapid identification of compounds and events, and selections are made by simply checking boxes.

        • Search Drug Trial Information



          Review drug trial information, aggregated together from multiple sources, presented in a searchable and sortable table. Select a drug trial and click View and learn more about the results of the trial and the source of the data.

        • Add Experimentally Derived Assay Study Data



          Design and setup a study, add chip treatment information, then upload assay data and use the online tools to visualize, analyze, and computationally model the results. The MPS-Db accommodates assay data from all types of MPS organ models and assays (e.g., biosensors, ELISA, high content imaging, mass spec analysis).

        • Analyze Experimentally Derived Assay Study Data



          All MPS-Db users can access the built-in tools to analyze and model thier data, data that have been shared with them, and/or that have been opened to the public. The MPS-Db provides tools for analyzing and visualizing assay data and related images and videos. Data are typically grouped into studies comprised of a set of similarly prepared chips with an experimentally defined set of treatments. Assay results can be analyzed within a study (intra-study) or across studies (inter-study).

        • Controlled Sharing of Experimentally Derived Assay Study Data


          Researchers providing experimental data determine when and with whom study data are shared. Data can be kept private, shared within an institution, shared with a customized group of collaborators, opened to the public, and/or opened through a process of sign-off, stakeholder acknowledgement, and embargo.

        • Customized Data Sets for Use in Plots, Analysis, and Modeling



          The database includes a comprehensive interface for filtering and grouping data, and provides customizable tools for visualizing data. Data can be grouped by user selected parameters to facilitate comparisons, such as treated vs untreated tissue chips, and filters can be applied to limit data included in visualizations or used in analysis and modeling. In addition to options provided in the sidebar, more options, such as log transformation and plotting as dose-response, are available by selecting (right click) an individual plot.

        • Learn About a Disease and Studies in the MPS-Db Relating to a Disease



          The disease portal summarizes the number of studies in the MPS-Db relating to a specific disease, and provides links with additional information about the disease, including a disease overview, disease biology, clinical data associated with the disease, and links to studies relating to the disease.

Frequently Asked Questions

    • Registration is easy and free. Just go to the MPS login page and click on the option to register, or just click here.
    • The MPS-Db Help page provides detailed information and step-by-step examples on how to use the features of the MPS-Db. Click on the Help tab on the home page or click here to visit the page.
    • Yes. The database offers multiple ways of looking at compound data. Chemical, biological, and clinical information is available through the Compounds icon. Some users like to review the compound list, and sort, for example, by LogP. Then, use the global search to find additional information about promising compounds (such as, drug trials associated with a specific compound).
    • The MPS-Db code is freely available to nonprofit researchers or institutions and will be made available to for-profit corporations for a nominal license fee. For more information, send an email to MPSHELP@pitt.edu with "For-profit Use of the MPS-Db" in the subject line.
    • To add assay data to the database, you must be a registered user, assigned to a Data Group and granted the permissions needed to add and edit studies in that Data Group. These permissions must be configured by an MPS-Db Administrator. Contact an administrator by sending an email to MPSHELP@pitt.edu with "Getting Set Up to Contribute Data to the MPS-Db" in the subject line. Detailed information on how to review and analyze data in the MPS-Db is available on the database's Help page.
    • Data contributors are not required by the MPS-Db Center to share their data. Studies that have been shared publicly, or for which you are designated as a Data Group member or Collaborator, will show on the Study List Page of the database. These studies can also be accessed through the inter-study analysis feature and the disease portal, and when reviewing the Target/Analytes and Method/Kits lists (available through the Studies->Study Components menu). Review the Help page to learn about these features.
    • Data “stakeholders” are designated by the data contributor as users that have a vested interest in the MPS Model or data, such as a model developer. Stakeholders have the opportunity to review studies for which they have been designated as a stakeholder, following sign off by the data contributor and prior to the data being made accessible to any other user groups. To access data that are not yet publicly available, stakeholders must be registered MPS-Db users. Once registered, an MPS-Db Administrator will grant the permission needed for you to review and acknowledge that you have reviewed the relevant study data. The study (or studies) to be reviewed can be found on the Study List Page. Look for studies with a check mark, but without an ‘eye’ or a ‘pencil’ icon in the Review column.
    • The MPS-Db is funded by the National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences Tissue Chips Program, the National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences (NCATS) Tissue Chip Testing Centers Program, and in part by the Vanderbilt-Pittsburgh Resource for Organotypic Models for Predictive Toxicology.
    • The MPS-Db website is managed by the University of Pittsburgh Drug Discovery Institute). Additional information about the MPS-Db, included detailed instructions for using database features and contributing data to the database, can be found on the Help page. Questions and comments should be directed to MPSHELP@pitt.edu.
